The Canonical Plays of William Shakespeare

A refined reference guide mapping the core themes, plot structures, and major figures of the dramatic catalog.

The Major Tragedies

Hamlet

c. 1599–1601

Prince Hamlet of Denmark seeks revenge for his father's murder by his uncle Claudius, who has usurped the throne and married Hamlet's mother. The drama serves as a profound investigation into existential dread, internal hesitation, madness, and moral corruption.

Key Figures: Hamlet, Claudius, Gertrude, Ophelia, Horatio, Polonius

Macbeth

c. 1606

Driven by a prophetic encounter with three weird sisters and spurred by his wife's fierce ambition, a Scottish general murders his king to claim the throne. His reign quickly dissolves into paranoid tyranny, guilt, and systematic slaughter.

Key Figures: Macbeth, Lady Macbeth, Banquo, Macduff, Duncan

Othello

c. 1603

The malicious ensign Iago systematically manipulates a celebrated Moorish general in the Venetian military into believing his innocent new bride, Desdemona, is unfaithful. A devastating psychological evaluation of jealousy, vulnerability, and deception.

Key Figures: Othello, Iago, Desdemona, Cassio, Emilia

King Lear

c. 1605–1606

An aging monarch abdicates power, dividing his realm among his daughters based on insincere public flattery. Stripped of structural authority, he is cast into a metaphorical and literal storm, confronting the raw, unadorned vulnerability of human nature.

Key Figures: King Lear, Cordelia, Goneril, Regan, Edgar, Edmund

Romeo and Juliet

c. 1595–1596

Two star-crossed lovers from warring noble families in Verona marry in secret. A series of volatile misunderstandings and structural constraints drives them to a double suicide, which ultimately resolves their families' ancient, destructive blood feud.

Key Figures: Romeo, Juliet, Mercutio, Tybalt, Friar Lawrence

Julius Caesar

c. 1599

Concerned that Caesar's unprecedented popularity will result in tyranny, a faction of high-ranking Roman senators orchestrates his assassination. The act plunges Rome into a complex ideological and military power struggle over the survival of the Republic.

Key Figures: Brutus, Mark Antony, Julius Caesar, Cassius

The Major Comedies

Twelfth Night

c. 1601–1602

Separated from her twin brother in a shipwreck, Viola disguises herself as a male page named Cesario in Illyria. She enters the service of Duke Orsino, initiating a chaotic comedic love triangle that examines gender roles and identity confusion.

Key Figures: Viola (Cesario), Olivia, Orsino, Malvolio, Feste

The Merchant of Venice

c. 1596–1597

A Venetian merchant defaults on a loan secured from an outsider moneylender, Shylock, who demands a literal pound of flesh as penalty. Classified historically as a comedy due to its marriage framework, the text functions as a complex commentary on justice and mercy.

Key Figures: Shylock, Portia, Antonio, Bassanio

A Midsummer Night's Dream

c. 1595–1596

Four young lovers and a troupe of amateur working-class actors wander into an enchanted forest outside Athens. They become caught in a volatile domestic dispute between the fairy king and queen, mediated by magical love juices and mischievous pranks.

Key Figures: Puck, Oberon, Titania, Nick Bottom, Helena, Lysander

Much Ado About Nothing

c. 1598–1599

Features a dual narrative structure: a pair of cynical wits are tricked into confessing their hidden love through a 'merry war' of banter, while an adversarial plot seeks to destroy a second, traditional marriage through structural slander at the altar.

Key Figures: Beatrice, Benedick, Claudio, Hero, Don John

As You Like It

c. 1599

Fleeing court persecution, the resourceful Rosalind adopts a male identity and escapes into the pastoral Forest of Arden. The setting becomes an experimental space for testing romantic conventions, exploring philosophy, and restoring political order.

Key Figures: Rosalind (Ganymede), Orlando, Jaques, Celia

The Main Histories

Henry IV, Part 1

c. 1596–1597

As King Henry IV faces an organized domestic rebellion, his heir Prince Hal engages in youthful rebellion in London taverns alongside rowdy lowlifes. The narrative traces Hal's maturation into a credible leader on the battlefield at Shrewsbury.

Key Figures: Prince Hal, Sir John Falstaff, King Henry IV, Hotspur

Henry V

c. 1599

Focuses on the newly crowned King Henry V as he leaves his rowdy past behind to unite England and launch a highly calculated military campaign against France, culminating in an underdog victory at the historic Battle of Agincourt.

Key Figures: King Henry V, Chorus, Princess Katherine

Richard III

c. 1592–1593

Chronicles the manipulative, dark ascent of the physically deformed Duke of Gloucester as he plots, betrays, and assassinates his way to the English crown, establishing a short-lived tyranny that marks the conclusion of the Wars of the Roses.

Key Figures: Richard III, Queen Elizabeth, Lady Anne, Richmond

The Late Romances / Tragicomedies

The Tempest

c. 1610–1611

The usurped Duke of Milan, Prospero, uses magical control and spirits on an isolated island to conjure a storm that shipwrecks his old enemies. Rather than exacting absolute revenge, he uses the event to engineer political reconciliation and voluntarily renounce his magic.

Key Figures: Prospero, Ariel, Caliban, Miranda, Ferdinand

The Winter's Tale

c. 1609–1611

A Sicilian king falls into sudden, unprompted paranoia, destroying his family and causing his infant daughter to be abandoned in a remote region. Over a sixteen-year span, the narrative shifts from raw tragedy to pastoral comedy, culminating in a famous scene of magical restoration.

Key Figures: Leontes, Hermione, Paulina, Perdita, Florizel
